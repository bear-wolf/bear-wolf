For twelve years, Dr. Simon Wells, a well-respected nephrologist,
had run a successful practice in Long Island, New York, and had
never experienced any dissatisfaction amongst his staff. He was
proud that he had never fired any of his employees, and that the
majority of his team had been with him for five or more years. But,
why would four billing employees quit within two weeks, without
any reasonable explanation? Dr. Wells worried that somehow
he was to blame for their departure, and the uncertainty of the
situation made him anxious. Consequently, he and
Cheryl had spent most of the past week trying
to uncover the cause of the problem.

“Three of our staff members
in yesterday’s focus group
discussion complained
about prior authorizations,”
said Cheryl.

“We’ll just have to wait until we get the results of the employee
satisfaction questionnaire we distributed,” said Dr. Wells.

“There’s also that research your friend’s son is doing…” said Cheryl.

“It will be interesting to see what he digs up,” said Dr. Wells,
“although, I’m not sure I should be relying on a college
student for advice on how to run my practice.”

“I wish there was someone whose advice we could trust,”
Cheryl sighed.

At that moment, a recent conversation popped into Dr. Wells’ head.
His friend and colleague, Dr. George Osborne, had mentioned a
business consultant who had helped him in the past.

Dr. Wells called George, who gave him the consultant’s number, and
within a week, Dr. Wells had set up a meeting with Wendy Berman.
