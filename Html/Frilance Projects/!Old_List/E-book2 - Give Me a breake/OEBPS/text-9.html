A Different Kind of Staff Meeting

Dr. Wells called a mandatory staff meeting before his first patient
on Friday morning. All 16 staff members gathered
in the waiting room, including the billing clerks,
nurses, physician assistants, and receptionists.
Dr. Wells noticed a palpable tension in the air.
It may have been the early hour, but the stillness
in the room felt like an indicator of something
unpleasant to come. Dr. Wells
broke the silence.

“Good morning, everyone. I’d like
you all to meet Wendy Berman,
a business consultant we’ve
brought in to help us solve
a problem. I’ve asked you
to come together because
I’m concerned about what
seems to be a negative
mood circulating around
the office lately. As you all
know, four members of our staff
have recently left us. I’m proud of
the practice that Cheryl and I started
together 12 years ago, and I want to
continue to see it grow and prosper.

So, I’d be very grateful if all of you would help us brainstorm the
root cause of the problem.”

Wendy explained that brainstorming is a way to generate ideas and
spur creativity. Every idea gets written down, and none of the ideas
are rejected or evaluated until the brainstorming process is over.

“Can we be totally honest without any repercussions, Dr. Wells?”
asked one brave receptionist.

“Absolutely! Don’t hold back; we need to hear your real thoughts
and feelings.”
