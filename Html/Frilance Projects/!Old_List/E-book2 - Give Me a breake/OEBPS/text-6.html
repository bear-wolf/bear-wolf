Consider the Source

“In the meantime,” said Wendy, “let’s review the
information that your friend’s son has gathered.”

Dr. Wells handed Wendy a professionally
bound portfolio of photocopied articles
and news clips. After paging through it
for a few minutes, Wendy turned
to Dr. Wells with a smile.

“It would be a great learning experience
for your friend’s son if you could go through
this research with him and
show him which sources
are reliable, and which
are not,” she said.

Dr. Wells laughed. “At least he isn’t using
People magazine,” he said, “but I do have
my doubts about Wikipedia.”

“I use five criteria for evaluating
information,” said Wendy “Accuracy,
Authority, Currency, Objectivity, and
Relevance. We can assume that
information from peer-reviewed
journals and respected news
organizations has been
checked for Accuracy.

That’s where Authority comes in – established and well-respected
sources are more credible, as are authors with proven expertise.
I always encourage everyone to search for the most Current
information available, and, of course, it must be both
objective and relevant.”

“When evaluating websites,” continued Wendy, “it
can sometimes be difficult to discern whether or not
the information is valid and reliable. One helpful hint is
to look at how the web address ends. Typically speaking,
websites that end in .edu or .gov contain information that can
be considered reliable. Many websites ending in .com contain
useful and reliable information as well, but be sure take note
of the author of the website. If there are errors in spelling or
grammar, or the page looks unprofessional, that should raise
a red flag. The information may not be completely accurate,
or may simply be someone’s opinion.”

Wendy asked Dr. Wells to
get together with Cheryl
later to brainstorm some
more sources of information
about job satisfaction and
to determine whether each
one met the five criteria:
Accuracy, Authority, Currency,
Objectivity, and Relevance.
